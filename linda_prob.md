Here is the [Linda problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conjunction_fallacy#:~:text=The%20conjunction%20fallacy%20(also%20known,a%20type%20of%20formal%20fallacy.): 
Linda is 31 years old, single, outspoken, and very bright. She majored in philosophy. As a student, she was deeply concerned with issues of discrimination and social justice, and also participated in anti-nuclear demonstrations.

Which is more probable?
Linda is a bank teller.
Linda is a bank teller and is active in the feminist movement.

Is someone who answers (2) guilty of a probabilistic fallacy? I argue that they are not.

The validity of the claim that those participants who select option two are committing a
probabilistic fallacy relies upon the equivocation of the colloquial use of “probable” with the
formal theory of probability as defined by the standard axioms. Once this leap is made, it is clear
how the conclusion is drawn. The set of entities for which the conjunction A and B holds is a
proper subset of those for which A holds, granted there exists some A and not B. The conflation
of terminology though is inappropriate, as demonstrated by the results of a similar experiment
where the problem is phrased as a question of frequency. In this framing, the intended response
is found consistently, signaling some intrinsic difference in the phrasing of the problem.

One possible explanation is that the use of “frequency” is a priming phrase that alerts the
listener to the technical context of the question, and hints that the intended manner to arrive at
a solution is quantitative or mathematical. Without this guidepost, the identification of the
question’s domain is ambiguous, despite the inclusion of “probable” as a key word. Therefore, it
is reasonable for the respondent to assume the appropriate context is an informal exchange,
where the notion of “probable” and “likely” are often taken apart from their formal definitions.

Given that the appropriate context is one of informal exchange, the natural approach
through which to interpret the question is entirely separate from, although not immediately
opposed to, probabilistic reasoning. Critics of Tarkovsky and Kahneman have referenced an
aspect of social scientific theory as explanatory of the phenomenon, Grice’s conversational
[maxims](https://www.sas.upenn.edu/~haroldfs/dravling/grice.html). These maxims are taken as common understandings which underpin the effective
relay of information. In the instance of the Linda problem, the appropriate maxim is the maxim
of quantity, which states that the information shared should be necessary and sufficient for the
intended effect. In observance of this principle, the participant is significantly more likely to put
material weight on the initial description of Linda as being necessary to the response. Therefore,
the inclusion of the initial description of Linda is misleading, for within a conversational context
it is generally impolite to provide copious details which are irrelevant to the topic of discussion.
Out of trust in the humanity of the surveyors and expectation of their adherence to these
principles of conversation, the seemingly irrational behavior of concluding Linda is a bank teller
and a feminist quite reasonable.

In summary, the argument runs as follows. The application of the axioms of probability
to this problem is inappropriate as the use of “probable” does not sufficiently signal the
expectation that the question is to be considered in a mathematical context, and therefore the
reliance on standard conversational norms is justified. One the problem is framed as an
informal exchange, it follows that it adheres to the set of standard behaviors which are necessary
for effective communication, namely Grice’s maxims. Participants rely on the maxim of quantity,
which states that only relevant information is to be supplied when communicating an idea.
Then, when selecting which characterization is more probable, the participant finds that the
second option incorporates the lengthy description of Linda, and consequently is explanatory of
the inclusion of those details. Therefore, those who answer with response two are not
committing a probabilistic fallacy.
